1. Executive Summary
The Practice-Area Depth Index (PADI) is a diagnostic and engineering framework designed to transition legal digital assets from legacy keyword dependency to machine-validated legal doctrine modeling. In high-competition metropolitan markets, such as Personal Injury in Dallas, Texas, traditional SEO visibility is increasingly fragmented. PADI v1.0 addresses this by establishing an enforceable structural completeness standard rooted in W3C-normative technologies.
By implementing PADI, a Legal Service Provider (LSP) shifts from "surface-level metadata" to Jurisdictionally Reinforced Semantic Dominance, ensuring that AI agents and search engines recognize the entity not just as a "result," but as a verified Authority-Dominant node.

2. Introduction: The Shift to Authority Architecture
The Post-Information Age has rendered "content volume" an insufficient metric for competitive dominance. Modern search ecosystems and Large Language Models (LLMs) increasingly rely on Knowledge Graphs and Structured Data to verify the legitimacy of professional services.
2.1 The Problem: Structural Invisibility
Most legal digital assets suffer from "Fragmented Authority" (PADI Tier 8–15), where case subtypes and injury taxonomies are modeled without doctrinal depth. This results in "Structural Invisibility," where an entity's expertise is unreadable to machine crawlers.
2.2 The Solution: The PADI Framework
PADI v1.0 introduces a four-layer implementation strategy to remediate these gaps:
Layer 1 (Ontological): Establishing a formal OWL 2 authority architecture.
Layer 2 (Structured Data): Mapping ontological definitions to Schema.org vocabulary for search engine ingestion.
Layer 3 (Content Architecture): Structuring human-readable landing pages to mirror the underlying machine-readable logic.
Layer 4 (Authority Reinforcement): Integrating trust signals and jurisdictional citations to satisfy the Scoring Framework.

3. Normative Foundations
To ensure global interoperability and machine trust, PADI v1.0 strictly adheres to the following standards:
OWL 2 Web Ontology Language: For defining the logic of legal relationships.
SHACL (Shapes Constraint Language): For enforcing mandatory data shapes and cardinality (e.g., the $\ge 3$ Case Subtype requirement).
JSON-LD 1.1: For the delivery of structured metadata.
4. Core Terminology
To ensure semantic interoperability, PADI v1.0 defines the following foundational entities:
4.1 Legal Service Provider (LSP): An entity offering legally regulated services within a defined jurisdiction.
4.2 Practice Area: A formally recognized domain of legal representation (e.g., Personal Injury).
4.3 Case Subtype: A structured specialization within a practice area (e.g., Commercial Trucking Accidents).
4.4 Legal Mechanism: A doctrinal requirement necessary to establish liability (e.g., Proximate Cause).
4.5 Jurisdictional Reinforcement: The explicit structural linkage to local statutes, courts, and geographic boundaries.
4.6 Structural Authority: The measurable completeness of an entity's legal modeling across all PADI-defined taxonomies.

5. Architectural Requirements
PADI-Compliant systems must adhere to the following mandatory structural constraints to ensure machine readability and competitive dominance.
5.1 Core Entity Clarity
A Legal Service Provider MUST:
Explicitly declare their operating jurisdiction.
Maintain a structured identity (via JSON-LD) that resolves to a verifiable real-world entity.
Model all service relationships using the PADI OWL Ontology.
5.2 Practice-Area Depth
To avoid "Structural Invisibility," each Practice Area MUST:
Include $\ge 3$ distinct case subtypes.
Provide a unique landing page and metadata node for each subtype.
5.3 Injury & Damages Taxonomy
Each Case Subtype MUST model the following to satisfy the Scoring Framework:
Include $\ge 3$ specific injury types (e.g., Traumatic Brain Injury, Spinal Cord Damage).
Model both Economic and Non-Economic damage categories.
5.4 Legal Mechanism Modeling (Negligence)
For Personal Injury implementations, the system MUST model the four pillars of negligence doctrine:
Duty.
Breach.
Causation.
Damages.
Warning: The absence of any required doctrinal element constitutes a Critical Violation, resulting in a "Structurally Invisible" tier assignment.
5.5 Jurisdictional Anchoring
The architecture MUST anchor the entity to its local legal environment by identifying:
Governing state and local statutes.
Relevant courts of jurisdiction (e.g., Dallas County District Courts).
Applicable Statute of Limitations for the modeled case subtypes.

6. Trust & Conversion Layer
Competitive dominance requires that trust signals be machine-readable. Implementation MUST include:
$\ge 1$ Structured review aggregate.
$\ge 1$ Professional credential signal (e.g., Board Certification).
Structured modeling of specific case results/past outcomes.
9. Compliance & Enforcement (SHACL)
PADI v1.0 utilizes Shapes Constraint Language (SHACL) to automate the validation of legal authority. A system is deemed PADI-Compliant only if all mandatory shapes validate without violation.
9.1 Mandatory Validation Domains
The PADI Auditor executes the following shape checks against the entity’s RDF graph:
Practice Area Shape: Validates the presence of $\ge 3$ case subtypes.
Case Subtype Shape: Validates the presence of $\ge 3$ injury types, $\ge 2$ damage types, and $\ge 1$ legal mechanism.
Negligence Shape: Enforces the "Four Pillars" requirement (Duty, Breach, Causation, Damages).
Jurisdiction Shape: Validates the presence of $\ge 1$ governing statute and $\ge 1$ court.
9.2 Enforcement Severity Levels
Omissions are categorized by their impact on machine-readability:
Warning: Recommended element missing (e.g., secondary trust signal).
Violation: Mandatory requirement missing (e.g., failing the 3-subtype rule).
Critical: Doctrinal incompleteness (e.g., missing "Causation" modeling). This results in an automatic "Structurally Invisible" classification.

10. The PADI Scoring Framework
The total PADI Score (0–25) is derived from five primary domains, each weighted equally at 5 points:
Domain
Criteria for 5/5 Score
Core Entity Clarity
Verified URI, declared jurisdiction, and RDF relationship modeling.
Subtype Coverage
Full modeling of $\ge 3$ distinct case subtypes within the practice area.
Injury & Damages Depth
Comprehensive taxonomy of physical, economic, and non-economic harm.
Legal Mechanism
100% doctrinal completeness (Duty, Breach, Causation, Damages).
Jurisdictional Reinforcement
Explicit links to city/state statutes and local court entities.


11. Tier Classification
Based on the cumulative score, entities are assigned a Competitive Tier:
Structurally Invisible (0–7): Minimal modeling; unreadable to advanced AI agents.
Fragmented Authority (8–15): Partial coverage; significant "Authority Gaps" present.
Competitive but Vulnerable (16–22): Strong modeling but lacks full jurisdictional reinforcement.
Authority-Dominant (23–25): The PADI Gold Standard. Comprehensive, jurisdictionally-anchored dominance.
